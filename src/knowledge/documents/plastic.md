# Plastic Waste — Disposal & Handling Guide

## Resin Codes and Recyclability

Plastic #1 PET (polyethylene terephthalate) is used in water bottles, soda bottles, and food containers. It is one of the most widely recycled plastics globally. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable. Bin: blue. Priority: high.

Plastic #2 HDPE (high-density polyethylene) is used in milk jugs, detergent bottles, shampoo bottles, and plastic bags. It is widely accepted in curbside recycling programs. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable. Bin: blue. Priority: high.

Plastic #3 PVC (polyvinyl chloride) is found in pipes, window frames, and some food wrap. It is rarely accepted in curbside recycling and can contaminate recycling streams. It may release toxic chlorine compounds when incinerated. CleanBot action: collect_landfill. Bin: black. Priority: medium.

Plastic #4 LDPE (low-density polyethylene) is used in plastic bags, squeezable bottles, and plastic wrap. Most curbside programs do not accept it, but many grocery stores have drop-off bins for plastic bags. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable. Bin: blue (store drop-off). Priority: medium.

Plastic #5 PP (polypropylene) is used in yogurt containers, medicine bottles, bottle caps, and straws. It is increasingly accepted in curbside programs. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable. Bin: blue. Priority: high.

Plastic #6 PS (polystyrene / Styrofoam) is used in foam cups, takeout containers, and packing material. It is rarely recyclable curbside. It persists in the environment for 500+ years and breaks into microplastics that enter waterways. CleanBot action: collect_landfill. Bin: black. Priority: high — high environmental impact if left on ground.

Plastic #7 is a catch-all for all other plastics including polycarbonate and bioplastics. Recyclability varies. Do not assume recyclable. CleanBot action: collect_landfill unless local facility confirms otherwise. Bin: black.

## Plastic Bags and Film

Plastic bags and plastic film wrapping are not accepted in standard recycling bins because they jam sorting machinery. They should be taken to grocery store drop-off locations. On the ground they are high-priority items because they block storm drains and are easily ingested by wildlife. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable (store drop-off). Priority: high.

## Plastic Bottle Caps

Bottle caps smaller than a credit card often fall through sorting machinery. Keep caps on bottles when recycling. Metal caps and plastic caps should stay on their respective bottles. CleanBot action: collect_recyclable (cap-on bottle). Priority: medium.

## Plastic Straws

Plastic straws are too small for most recycling machinery. They should be placed in the landfill bin. Straws in waterways are a documented threat to marine animals. CleanBot action: collect_landfill. Bin: black. Priority: high near water.

## Styrofoam / Expanded Polystyrene

Expanded polystyrene (EPS/Styrofoam) absorbs contaminants and breaks into persistent microplastic beads. Most recycling facilities do not accept it. Some municipalities have special foam drop-off programs. CleanBot action: collect_landfill. Bin: black. Priority: high — do not leave on ground.

## Contamination Risk

Plastic containers with food residue contaminate entire recycling loads. Ideally they should be rinsed before recycling. CleanBot cannot rinse items, so it should still collect them — contaminated plastics are better in a bin than in the environment.

## Hazard Level

Most plastics are low-hazard during collection. Exception: medical plastic (syringes, IV bags) is biohazardous and CleanBot should flag it for human pickup rather than collecting it directly. Contaminant risk: low for standard plastic, high for medical plastic.
